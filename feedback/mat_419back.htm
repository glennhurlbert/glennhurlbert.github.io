 2  1  Suggestions  Regarding 2.1.7 and 2.1.9 (among others), I think that if students are going to be asked to work unsolvable problems, it should be explicitly stated (at least in the introduction) that they may run into these types of problems in the text. As a student, it is tremendously frustrating to work on a problem in an area for which intuition and insight are not really developed yet and not be able to produce an answer. Of course, in research this is common, but the context is different and noone is too surprised when they are unable to do something. In text-book exercises it is assumed that an answer can be found, and if a student is unable to produce it, the automatic response is that she believes she is doing something wrong. There is, however, the danger that in stating up front that some problems will yield no solution, some students will use this as a way out of really trying on the difficult problems, assuming that if the answer is not immediately clear, the solu! tion must not exist. A suggestion, then, might be to change the wording of these types of problems. For example, 2.1.7 might read, "Is it possible to use the drawing to find the maximum? Why or why not?" I believe this still gets the student thinking in the desired direction, while avoiding the possibility that she will have the right idea (in this case, that the drawing is not enough to solve the LOP) and be convinced that she has done something wrong.
