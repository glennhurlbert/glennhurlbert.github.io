Zero-Sums
The origins of graph pebbling reside in combinatorial number theory and group theory. A sequence of elements of a finite group G is called a zero-sum sequence if it sums to the identity of G. A simple pigeonhole argument (on the sequence of partial sums) proves the following statement.

Pigeonhole Fact   Any sequence of |G| elements of a finite group G contains a zero-sum subsequence.

In fact, a subsequence of consecutive terms can be guaranteed by the pigeonhole argument. Furthermore, one can instead stipulate that the zero-sum subsequence has at most N terms, where N=N(G) is the exponent of G (i.e. the maximum order of an element of G), and this is best possible.

EGZ Theorem
Initiated in 1956 by Erdos [1], the study of zero-sum sequences has a long history with many important applications in number theory and group theory. In 1961 Erdos et al. [2] proved the following theorem, which we call the EGZ (Erdos-Ginzburg-Ziv) Theorem.

EGZ Theorem   Every sequence of 2|G|-1 elements of a cyclic group G contains a zero-sum subsequence of length exactly |G|.

In 1969 van Emde Boas and Kruyswijk [3] proved that any sequence of N(1+log(|G|/N)) elements of a finite abelian group contains a zero-sum sequence. In 1994 Alford et al. [4] used this result and modified Erdos's arguments to prove that there are infinitely many Carmichael numbers.

Davenport's Constant
Much of the recent study has involved finding Davenport's constant D(G), defined to be the smallest D such that every sequence of D elements contains a zero-sum subsequence [5]. There are a wealth of results on this problem {[6],[7],[8],[9],[10],[11]} and its variations {[12],[13]}.
Pebbling Birth

In 1989 Kleitman and Lemke [14], and independently Chung [15], proved the following theorem (originally stated number-theoretically), strengthening the Pigeonhole Fact. Let Zn denote the cyclic group of order n, and let |g| denote the order of an element g in the group to which it belongs.

Theorem 1   For every sequence (g_k)_{k=1}^n of n elements from Zn, there is a zero-sum subsequence (g_k)_{k \in K} such that /sum_{k \in K}1/|g_k| <= 1.

For a sequence S the sum $\sum_{g\in S}1/|g|$ is known as the cross number of S and is an important invariant in factorization theory. Guaranteeing cross number at most 1 strengthens the extension of the Pigeonhole Fact that |K|<=N(G), and shows that equality holds if and only if every |gk|=N. The concept of pebbling in graphs arose from an attempt by Lagarias and Saks to give an alternative (and more natural and structural) proof than that of Kleitman and Lemke; it was Chung who carried out their idea. See also [16] for another extension of this result.

Kleitman and Lemke then conjectured that Theorem 1 holds for all finite groups. For a subgroup H of G, call a sequence of elements of G an H-sum sequence if its elements sum to an element of H. In [17],[18] is proved the following theorem (the methods of [17] use graph pebbling).

Theorem 2   Let H be a subgroup of a finite abelian group G with |G|/|H|=n. For every sequence $(g_k)_{k=1}^n$ of n elements from G there is an H-sum subsequence $(g_k)_{k\in K}$ such that $\sum_{k\in K}1/|g_k|\le 1/|\sum_{k\in K}\ g_k|$.

The case H={e} here gives Theorem 1 for finite abelian groups, strengthening the van Emde Boas and Kruyswijk result [3]. Kleitman and Lemke also conjectured that Theorem 2 holds for all finite groups, and verified their conjecture for all dihedral groups (see [14]). For other nonabelian groups, it has been shown recently to hold for the nonabelian solvable group of order 21 (see [17]).

Olson's Conjecture
It would be interesting to see whether graph pebbling methods can shed light on the Davenport constant for finite abelian groups. In this regard, one of the most pressing questions is as follows. Write G=\prod_{i=1}^r Zni, where 1 < n1|n2|...|nr. Then r=r(G) is the rank of G. It is natural to guess that D(G)=\sum_{i=1}^r ni-r+1=1+\sum_{i=1}^r (ni-1) from pigeonhole intuition. This was conjectured By Olson in [5] and is true by Theorem 1 for rank 1 groups. Moreover, it was proven in [5] for rank 2 groups and p-groups as well. However, it was proven in [3],[10] that the conjecture is false for some groups each rank at least 4. What remains open is the instance of rank 3.
Applications
There are applications in factorization theory [19] and graph theory [20].
